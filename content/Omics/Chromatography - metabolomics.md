[[Analytical techniques]]
Depending on the properties of the metabolites, different stationary and mobile phases have to be used to achieve separation of the analyte. There is a technique for each property but not one, that works for all analytes (not all chromatography's are compatible with MS)![[Metabolomics2.pdf#page=30&rect=55,35,772,502|Metabolomics2, p.30]]
## Gas chromatography (GS)
GS is normally used for volatile analytes, but a wider range of metabolites can be analysed, if they undergo prior derivation. Derivation of a compound lowers its boiling point by e.g. sililation.![[Metabolomics2.pdf#page=33&rect=438,41,804,339|Metabolomics2, p.33]]GS is suitable for small analytes (<500 DA), that undergo hard ionization and rich fragmentation. Low resolution MS are sufficient (and MS/MS isn't necessary), since there are known retention time indexes, and standardized MS libraries.
GS mainly uses one column (DB-5), since the molecular properties of the analytes are fairly similar after derivation.
## Liquid chromatography (LC)
In contrast to GC LC is able to separate much bigger molecules, that don't have to be derivatized. Soft ionization is used in conjunction with LC and higher resolution MS are required. These factors result in more complex data, and no real standardization. There are some references, but they are less comprehensive than for GC. 
There are more columns available for LC, and they can be separated into **reverse phase** and **hydrophilic** chromatographic columns. Hydrophobic metabolites are analysed with reverse phase chromatography, which has a hydrophilic stationary phase and polar mobile phase. Where hydrophilic metabolites are separated with a polar stationary phase and non-polar mobile phase in a hydrophilic interaction chromatography.
The sequence of peaks of the same analyte is reversed between the two![[Metabolomics2.pdf#page=37&rect=404,18,719,246|Metabolomics2, p.37]]
